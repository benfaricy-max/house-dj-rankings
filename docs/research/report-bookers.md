# PEAKTIME (thedjrankings.com) — Signal Value for Bookers/Promoters Analysis Report

## Executive Summary
This research investigated how electronic music bookers and promoters evaluate artist demand, and how they perceive the utility of PEAKTIME's proprietary signals (Value Gap, Scene Score, Momentum, Reach vs Credibility). The findings reveal that while bookers are highly receptive to data that provides negotiation leverage—particularly the 'Value Gap' signal—they are deeply skeptical of global metrics that lack local context. Scene credibility strictly outweighs mainstream reach, as promoters prioritize protecting their venue's authentic vibe over chasing viral hype. To successfully monetize PEAKTIME at the expected £75/month solo or £300/month team price points, the platform must bridge the gap between global rankings and local realities by integrating real-time routing data and local ticket velocity.

## Research Overview
*   **Study Name**: PEAKTIME (thedjrankings.com) — Signal Value for Bookers/Promoters
*   **Methodology**: 1-on-1 video interviews
*   **Sample Size**: 4 participants
*   **Fieldwork Dates**: June 4, 2026
*   **Target Audience**: Bookers and promoters who have personally booked 3+ electronic acts in the past 12 months in the US or UK.
*   **Core Focus**: Identifying the most trusted signals for booking decisions, pressure-testing the 'Value Gap' against real-world fee knowledge, and determining willingness-to-pay for actionable fee intelligence.

## Key Findings

**Finding 1: Local ticket velocity and routing context are the ultimate validators for any platform signal.**
Global metrics like streams or social followers are viewed as noise by bookers. To be trusted, PEAKTIME signals must correlate with local presales, regional routing gaps, and on-the-ground scene chatter. Without local context, bookers will not override their own spreadsheets or peer networks, as the financial risk of a failed booking is too high.
*   "I trust presales because they directly tie to cash flow and demand in my room—hard numbers, no guesses." — Interview 1 [A]
*   "Honestly, ticket sales velocity and local promoter feedback beat any algorithm. Social buzz is noise unless it lines up with on-the-ground proof." — Interview 3 [A]
*   "I’m wary of numbers without context, so I cross-check any data or hype with my gut and live experience..." — Interview 4 [B]
*   **Frequency**: Many
*   **Impact**: High
*   **Confidence**: High
*   **Supported by**: Interviews 1, 2, 3, 4
*   **Counterexamples**: None. All participants universally demanded local validation before trusting a digital signal.
*   **Markets**:
    *   NYC / US: Emphasized presale velocity and competing local events.
    *   UK: Emphasized routing saturation and regional promoter feedback.

**Finding 2: 'Value Gap' is highly desired as a negotiation lever against inflated agent quotes.**
Participants immediately understood Value Gap as a tool to spot underpriced talent. They intend to use it tactically to push back on agents, secure better splits, or open holds earlier, provided the platform's fee estimates align with their internal comps. It is viewed as the most actionable signal for direct financial ROI.
*   "Value gap basically means there’s demand for an artist outpacing their current booking fee... It’s a buy signal telling me I could lock in a good deal before fees spike" — Interview 1 [A]
*   "I’d definitely consider Value Gap as a strong signal... I’d reference it to support where my offers sit in the market, especially to push back against inflated quotes" — Interview 2 [B]
*   "Value Gap could push me to open negotiations earlier or set tighter guarantee caps knowing demand is rising." — Interview 3 [B]
*   **Frequency**: Many
*   **Impact**: High
*   **Confidence**: High
*   **Supported by**: Interviews 1, 2, 3, 4
*   **Counterexamples**: Interview 4 noted that Value Gap alone wouldn't trigger a booking without a vibe check, acting as a conversation starter rather than a final decider.
*   **Markets**: All identified markets echoed this utility for negotiation.

**Finding 3: Scene credibility strictly outweighs mainstream reach for protecting brand and room energy.**
Bookers prioritize authentic underground respect over broad commercial appeal. High reach without credibility is seen as a severe risk that could alienate core regulars, ruin the night's vibe, and damage the venue's long-term curation reputation. The 'Scene Score' is highly trusted when it cites tangible markers like Berghain, fabric, or Boiler Room.
*   "Credibility edges out reach for me—our crowd cares about authentic underground vibes." — Interview 1 [A]
*   "Credibility matters most for us because our regulars trust the venue’s curation... even if they don’t have massive mainstream reach." — Interview 2 [A]
*   "Credibility matters more — our brand’s about protecting taste and room energy, not just filling seats." — Interview 4 [A]
*   **Frequency**: Many
*   **Impact**: High
*   **Confidence**: High
*   **Supported by**: Interviews 1, 2, 3, 4
*   **Counterexamples**: Interview 3 noted that reach is still considered for larger festival stages to satisfy sponsors, but credibility remains the baseline.
*   **Markets**: All identified markets echoed this prioritization.

**Finding 4: 'Momentum' is viewed with skepticism as it often reflects viral hype rather than reliable ticket draw.**
While useful for early discovery, momentum is the most distrusted signal out of the box. Bookers fear it captures superficial social spikes or one-off festival slots that do not translate into hard ticket sales in a club environment, making it a metric easily weaponized by agents to inflate fees.
*   "False positives might come from viral moments that don’t translate into real ticket sales or local interest—for example, a big Spotify playlist spike without credible club bookings behind it." — Interview 2 [A]
*   "False positives come from online hype that doesn’t convert live—like viral streams with no real fanbase yet." — Interview 3 [A]
*   "If I had to drop one, probably momentum. Not because it’s useless, but because it’s so easily gamed or exaggerated by agents trying to bump their fee." — Interview 4 [A]
*   **Frequency**: Many
*   **Impact**: Medium
*   **Confidence**: High
*   **Supported by**: Interviews 1, 2, 3, 4
*   **Counterexamples**: Interview 1 finds momentum useful for spotting acts before fees spike, provided it is cross-checked quickly.
*   **Markets**: All identified markets echoed this skepticism.

**Finding 5: Willingness-to-pay hinges on real-time integration with existing workflows and clear ROI.**
Solo bookers cap their willingness to pay around £50-100/month, while team plans stretch to £300-400. To justify the recurring cost, the tool must move beyond static dashboards and offer real-time alerts, routing integration, or direct fee cross-checking that demonstrably saves money during negotiations.
*   "If the tool truly saves me time and money, I’m happy to pay a Pro-level subscription around £50–£100 per month." — Interview 1 [A]
*   "Something around £300–£400 per month for a team plan would feel reasonable if the data clearly saves time or prevents costly booking mistakes." — Interview 2 [A]
*   "A no-brainer would be under £100/month for solo or small-team use—something that directly saves me time or money on dealmaking." — Interview 4 [A]
*   **Frequency**: Many
*   **Impact**: High
*   **Confidence**: High
*   **Supported by**: Interviews 1, 2, 3, 4
*   **Counterexamples**: None. All participants demanded clear ROI to justify subscription costs.
*   **Markets**: All identified markets aligned on these price thresholds.

## Themes & Patterns
*   **The Agent-Promoter Tug-of-War**: Promoters view agents as inherently biased, often inflating fees based on global hype or irrelevant market anchors. PEAKTIME is seen as a potential equalizer—an objective third-party data source that bookers can use to call bluffs and anchor negotiations in reality.
*   **The Danger of the "Viral" DJ**: There is a shared anxiety across all participants about booking artists who have exploded on TikTok or Spotify but lack the technical skill or scene respect to hold a dancefloor. High reach combined with low credibility is treated as a massive operational risk.

## Market Nuances
*   **NYC / US**: This market places a heavy emphasis on presale velocity and break-even thresholds in a highly competitive environment where competing local events can instantly steal momentum. The focus is on hard financial denominators to manage risk. "I had to renegotiate to $14k plus door splits and reshuffle marketing spend. Not foolproof, but better than overguaranteeing blind." — Interview 1 [B]
*   **UK**: This market focuses heavily on routing saturation, regional promoter networks, and avoiding acts that have overplayed nearby cities. Peer validation from local residents and northern promoters is critical to assessing true demand. "That taught me to factor in UK routing saturation more carefully and not rely solely on digital momentum." — Interview 2 [B]

## Personas (not evidenced)
While distinct behavioral patterns emerged—specifically a "Data-Driven ROI Maximizer" focused on presales and spreadsheets, and a "Vibe & Credibility Curator" focused on room energy and resident feedback—neither cluster reached the mandatory three-interview threshold required by the methodology. With only two participants leaning into each approach, the candidate personas failed the Coverage gate and could not be robustly validated against the Contrast gate.

## Opportunity Areas
*   **Real-Time Routing & Saturation Alerts**: Participants repeatedly asked for routing context. Integrating calendar data to show if an artist is overplaying a specific region (e.g., "Played 3 times in the UK in the last 60 days") would bridge the gap between global data and local trust.
*   **Negotiation Playbooks**: Packaging the Value Gap signal with historical comp data to give bookers a direct script or leverage point for agent emails. If PEAKTIME can provide the exact data points needed to counter an inflated quote, its ROI becomes undeniable.

## Recommendations
*   **Anchor Value Gap in Local Comps**: Ensure the Value Gap explicitly references venue size or regional fee averages, rather than a global abstract number. Bookers need to know the gap applies to their specific market capacity to trust it.
*   **Deprioritize or Recontextualize Momentum**: Add a "Club vs. Viral" filter to the Momentum signal so bookers can distinguish between genuine underground traction (e.g., rising club bookings) and superficial streaming spikes.
*   **Highlight Scene Score Inputs**: Make the specific inputs for Scene Score (e.g., Berghain, Boiler Room, specific labels) highly visible on the artist card. These tangible markers drive credibility trust and validate the platform's methodology.
*   **Target Pricing at £75/mo (Solo) and £300/mo (Team)**: Position the Pro tier around £75/month, emphasizing the direct ROI of saving money on a single inflated guarantee. Avoid charging for raw data; charge for the negotiation leverage.

## Limitations & Open Questions
*   **Sample Size**: The findings are based on a small sample size (N=4), which limits the ability to generalize across all tiers of promoters.
*   **Missing Screener Data**: The lack of formal screener data limits demographic and firmographic segmentation, relying entirely on self-reported context within the transcripts.
*   **Open Question**: How will booking agents react to promoters using PEAKTIME data in negotiations, and will they accept the platform's Value Gap as a valid market benchmark?

## Appendix B: Methodology Details
This report was generated using a rigorous qualitative synthesis methodology. Each transcript was coded for Behaviors [B], Usage [U], and Attitudes [A]. Findings were developed through thematic analysis and prioritized based on frequency across participants and impact on the user experience. Persona generation was attempted using a strict six-phase B/U/A framework, but was omitted due to insufficient cross-participant evidence.

## Appendix C: Full Codebook Reference
*   **[B] Behaviour**: Actions, workflows, decisions, negotiation tactics, and reliance on peer networks.
*   **[U] Usage**: How participants interact with data tools, frequency of checking comps, and desired feature integrations (e.g., routing alerts).
*   **[A] Attitude**: Core beliefs about scene credibility, skepticism toward viral momentum, and definitions of fair pricing.
